Longing for "La Ingrata"

Originally written in 2017

“La Ingrata” is a track off of Mexican rock band Café Tacuba’s 1994 sophomore album Re, and the song lyrically culminates in a feminicide. Translated in English as “The Ungrateful Woman,” the song ends with a man informing his female lover, whom he just shot to death, that he will be in mourning at her funeral. La Santa Cecilia along with the Chilean singer-songwriter Mon Laferte recently covered the song for the Mexican-American band’s visual album Amar y Vivir, which was recorded live across Mexico City over the span of five days. A selection of mostly well-known Mexican songs like “El Último Trago,” “Leña de Pirul,” and the eponymous “Amar y Vivir,” the album is an ode to Mexico specifically and Latin America generally. The presence of “La Ingrata” in La Santa Cecilia’s album confirms the song’s status as a standard of Mexican music; and as a standard, the cover establishes the normalization of misogyny throughout Mexico.

A year before Re was released, journalists began reporting on the murders of women and girls along the Mexico/US border, especially in Ciudad Juárez, Chihuahua. Since then, it has become evident that feminicides are a pervasive feature of Mexican life. According to a joint report released in 2016 by the Government of Mexico and the United Nations, there were approximately 47,178 feminicide victims in Mexico in a thirtyyear period (1985–2014).

Presently in Mexico, from Chihuahua to Chiapas, the feminicide rate is alarmingly high. Estado de México, the state that surrounds the majority of Mexico City, has been deemed “the most dangerous place to be female” given that between 2011 and 2012 1,258 women and girls were reported missing and 448 dead. The situation is so dire that municipalities across the country regularly issue what are called “gender alerts”—emergency declarations that are suppose to provide communities and law enforcement agencies with governmental resources with the aim of combatting feminicides. The ubiquitousness of feminicides led the Supreme Court of Mexico to rule in 2015 that any murder involving a woman or girl must be investigated as a possible feminicide.

Like feminicides, “La Ingrata” is pervasive and popular in Mexico since the 1990s. “La Ingrata” is as much characteristic of the contemporary Mexican songbook as telling of the standardization of violence faced by women, girls, and any feminine-read person. In Mexico as elsewhere, homophobia and transphobia are, after all, other forms misogyny takes—something Café Tacuba is well aware of. In fact in an interview with Argentine newspaper La Nación in late 2016, the Mexican rockers announced that they were considering whether to change the song’s lyrics or refrain from performing it altogether. Rubén Albarran, the lead singer of Café Tacuba, stated in the interview that it is precisely because a song is the manifestation of culture—inclusive of its practices and beliefs towards femininity—that he did not want to continue being an apologist for feminicides.

Performed as a ska punk parody of Mexico’s norteño music style, Café Tacuba’s version of “La Ingrata” is a spoof—affection in the guise of scorn or contempt masked as adoration, hard to tell. Regardless, there is a sense of resentment undergirding this ambiguity, which Rubén’s raspy and petulant voice relays to the female addressee of the song.

La Santa Cecilia and Mon Laferte’s cover, on the other hand, has lost the cranky tenor. Instead, the singers voices are full of sorrow conveying more sadness than anger. Amplifying this anguish is the ranchera’s arrangement, which incorporates bolero and danzón—music genres known for inducing nostalgia and widely understood to be representative of traditional Mexican music. This most recent iteration of the song is then in some ways about the loss of a social sense of loss. In other words, the nostalgia projected and produced by the singers approximates a longing for a longing for those lives who have been lost to gender-based violence.

Shot on a roof of a building next to Mexico City’s Zócalo (main square), arguably the symbolic heart of the country, the music video is ostensibly a musical performance of what currently ails the Mexican social body. In La Santa Cecilia’s cover of “La Ingrata,” the singers’ smoky yet firm voices can be heard as sonic apparitions of feminicide victims. The bitter sadness of the song induces lament not only for those who have been silenced through feminicide but also for a society’s silence on misogyny.
