​An ode to the feature and "Humble Mumble" on the occasion of the 25th anniversary of the release of Stankonia

"Humble Mumble" quadtych, 2025

 

Originally written November 2025

In the universe of recorded popular music, songs with features, also referred to as collaborations, are the coming together of at least two musical acts for the recording of a track for an EP, full album, soundtrack, or single. Typically, one of the musical acts serves a principal role and the recording will be a part of their project while the additional acts take a secondary role as featured guests. Collaborations unseated the duet's prominence particulary in the U.S. where the latter's connotations to romance and femininity are interpreted as artistically limiting or threatening (not to all and to prove my points listen to Beyoncé and Miley Cyrus' "II MOST WANTED").

Like duets, features are cross-promotional opportunities—outreach marketing in commercial terms. Features' immediate appeal is audience growth as a vehicle towards larger profits. A more layered appreciation of features would mention too that as financially attuned recording practices these collaborations serve other creative aspirations as introductions, experiments, favors, reinventions as well as chances to impress (fans, artists, industry folk), express affinity (with the co-performer, a genre, a region), differentiate oneself (from past persona, allegiances, actions), rebel (from expectations), and expand upon an idea (think of remixes). And let's take a sentence to give Mariah Carey credit for all her contributions to the art of collaboration.

Outkast's "Humble Mumble," featuring Erykah Badu, is a proxy for Stankonia. Nestled in the second half of the album, the track, as a collaboration and like the rest of Stankonia, is an experimental endeavor. It is an effort to redefine Outkast and the sounds of (hip-hop in) the South. Quirky and free-spirited, the track leads with a synthesized "Latin" rhythm that to me seems to be a source of what becomes the hi-hat sound now definitive of trap. Then, about a third of the way into the track, and after Big Boi's verse, the beat changes with André 3000's verse. A funk-driven beat takes over, and Big Boi is not heard again.

"Humble Mumble" foreshadows the dissolution of the duo. The track's two-in-oneness is the musical model for the recording of the Speakerboxxx/The Love Below, a two-disc album on which Big Boi leans into Southern hip-hop in Speakerboxxx and André 3000 takes a more Prince/P-Funk-inspired musical turn in The Love Below. Erykah's feature on Stankonia also prefigures André 3000's collaborations with and reliance on women—Kelis, Norah Jones, Rosario Dawson, Qasha Aman, and Toni Hunter—in the The Love Below to establish his solo persona and offset any social doubts musical experimentations might suggest.

On Stankonia, Erykah Badu's featured verse in "Humble Mumble" might be the most radically unpretentious thing about the track as well as her musical repertoire to date. She offers an elemental meditation on life. Hers is a verse that is basic in its planetary singularity. And with that mix of simplicity and candor, Erykah achieves the nonconformity she so often reaches for musically. In "Humble Mumble," she sums up her musical pursuits, wails and all, and condenses the phases of existence as could only be done through a feature.
